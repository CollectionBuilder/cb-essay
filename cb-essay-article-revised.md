# Collaboration, Iteration, and Development: CB-Essay, a Static Web Template for Long-Form Multimodal Narratives

by Devin Becker and Evan Peter Williamson

## Abstract

Digital humanities scholarship increasingly demands publishing formats that move beyond the monograph or the simple digital exhibit, weaving long-form narrative writing together with the primary sources that evidence and animate scholarly arguments. Producing sustainable, accessible platforms for that kind of multimodal work remains hard. Traditional content management systems impose infrastructure burdens, customization challenges, and maintenance costs that undermine long-term access. Looking for an alternative, librarians at the University of Idaho's Center for Digital Inquiry and Learning (CDIL) have been developing CB-Essay, an open source static web template for publishing long-form multimodal essays integrated with digital collections of primary sources.

CB-Essay did not come out of a top-down design process. It developed through close collaboration between librarians and scholars across successive digital scholarship projects—a mode of development we argue is both more responsive to actual research needs and more likely to produce broadly reusable tools. Three projects shaped it: Fire Lines, which interweaves a creative nonfiction essay on the history of Idaho wildfires with a curated collection of historical fire records; Tender Spaces, a multilingual multimodal essay that draws on video interviews, photographs, and archival evidence to explore Gaëtane Buttigieg's life, art, and forced institutionalization in the 1970s; and Sedimentation, a multimedia exploration of Glen Canyon and the Glen Canyon Dam through interconnected scholarly essays and archival photographs. In each case, CDIL librarians worked iteratively with scholar-collaborators to prototype features that let text and collection items speak to one another, then refined and generalized those features until they coalesced into CB-Essay.

This report documents that process: how research needs drove template development, how each project contributed specific capabilities to the shared framework, and how the result lets new projects begin with a richer set of tools than any single collaboration could have produced on its own. We reflect on the collaboration model that made it possible—librarians working as both technical developers and intellectual partners—and on the static web foundation that made it affordable. CollectionBuilder, on which CB-Essay is built, supplies a minimal, durable infrastructure as a given; because the upkeep is low, the investment can go to scholarship and experiment instead of maintenance. The projects, in turn, taught us two things worth stating plainly: that collections are evidence and argument in their own right, not supplements to academic prose, and that the paths a reader can take between narrative and source are themselves a scholarly act.

## Article

Digital humanities scholarship increasingly demands publishing formats that go beyond what a monograph or a standard digital exhibit can offer. DH projects often call for long-form narrative writing that integrates primary source materials directly, weaving the archival record and the interpretive essay into a single reading experience. A scholar writing about a wildfire should be able to do more than cite an archival photograph in a Word document. She should be able to embed the photograph in her prose, let it interrupt and illuminate the argument, and let the reader dwell in the evidence. That is what multimodal scholarship looks like in practice, and that practice needs infrastructure to match.

The platforms usually recruited for this work—Scalar, WordPress, Omeka, Drupal—are content management systems built on dynamic web applications. They require server infrastructure, vigilant security updates, database maintenance, and a learning curve tied to a particular administrative interface. As Quinn Dombrowski has put it, adopting one of these systems is "like adopting a puppy that requires constant ongoing maintenance and attention" [Dombrowski 2019]. For projects whose active development spans a semester or a fellowship year and then slows, that maintenance burden is more than an inconvenience—it is a structural threat to long-term access. Features fail, links go dead, and sites become spam-bot factories. The infrastructure outlasts the resources to maintain it.

At the University of Idaho's Center for Digital Inquiry and Learning (CDIL), we have been working a different way: collaboratively building minimal, static web tools that keep infrastructure requirements low and keep the investment on content, data, and the intellectual work of the project. Out of that practice—specifically, out of three successive collaborations between CDIL librarians and graduate scholar-collaborators—grew CB-Essay, an open source static web template for publishing long-form multimodal essays integrated with digital collections of primary sources. This article documents how CB-Essay developed through collaborative ideation and iterative refinement, and what the resulting tool makes possible.

## CollectionBuilder and the Static Web Foundation

CB-Essay is built on [CollectionBuilder](https://collectionbuilder.github.io/), an open source framework for creating digital collection and exhibit websites developed at the University of Idaho Library. CollectionBuilder takes a minimal computing approach: rather than a CMS backed by a database, it uses a metadata CSV, a folder of digital media files, and Jekyll templates to generate a complete, self-contained static website. There is no server-side application to configure or maintain. The output is plain HTML, CSS, JavaScript, and media files, ready to deploy on any basic web server or free host like GitHub Pages.

The advantages of static web publishing are well documented [Wikle and Williamson 2025]. Static sites perform well without heavy infrastructure, keep working even without ongoing maintenance, and produce preservation-ready project data as a byproduct of the workflow. For digital scholarship at institutions with limited IT resources, these are not minor conveniences. They are often the difference between a project that endures and one that disappears.

In CollectionBuilder, metadata drives presentation: template logic parses the spreadsheet to produce collection visualizations, browse pages, item records, and data derivatives, and the framework provides templates for writing interpretive content that can pull in those item records through includes. To build CB-Essay, we inverted the emphasis. Instead of a collection-first site with optional interpretive content, CB-Essay puts long-form narrative writing at the center and integrates collection items in support of it, making the archival record available to the essay as evidence and illustration.

This is the foundation that makes everything else in this article possible, and it is worth being clear that CB-Essay does not invent it. The sustainability is inherited—from CollectionBuilder and from the static web approach itself. Because the infrastructure is low-cost and durable by default, the investment can go almost entirely into content, collaboration, and experiment rather than maintenance. A CB-Essay project, like any CollectionBuilder project, is finally just a folder of files: Markdown for essay chapters, a CSV of collection metadata, digital media, and the Jekyll templates that knit it together. That self-contained, plain-text, version-controlled structure is what allows rapid, agile iteration. Collaborators can learn the environment and the remixable recipes that create its features, which gives everyone room to explore.

## CB-Essay: What the Template Offers

[CB-Essay](https://github.com/CollectionBuilder/cb-essay) is available as an open source GitHub template. A new project starts by copying the repository, adding a collection metadata CSV and media files, writing essay content in Markdown, and configuring the Jekyll build. GitHub Pages, or any static host, can build and deploy the site with no server administration.

The reading experience is designed for long-form scholarship. The layout takes cues from Edward Tufte, with margin notes that link out to primary sources, Scrollama section transitions, and inline galleries that open multimedia items without leaving the page. Theming is flexible: three homepage layouts (full-screen banner, split layout, text-only), configurable typography, and eight color-and-font themes drawn from fine-press history—Kelmscott, Doves, Aldine, Nonesuch, and others. A Paged.js print hub turns the essay source files into properly typeset PDFs in several formats. And a GitHub Action can extract any of more than 60,000 Project Gutenberg texts into the essay folder, pre-split into chapters, for annotated editions or teaching.

Essays are authored in Markdown and sources live in CSV metadata. A dual-collection architecture keeps narrative content (the `_essay/` folder) separate from archival objects while letting the two integrate through objectid references. The standard CollectionBuilder pages—Browse, Map, Timeline, Subjects, full-text search, data downloads—sit alongside the essay, with no server, database, or hosting beyond GitHub Pages.

Because the data model is the same as CollectionBuilder's—Markdown content, CSV metadata, plain media files—CB-Essay projects are preservation-ready by default. There is no proprietary format and no platform lock-in. The source files are a complete, standards-based archival package that can be migrated to a future platform or transformed for a future inquiry, and the same files can produce a print version, enabling digital-first publication without redoing the authoring work.

## Three Projects, One Template

CB-Essay did not emerge from a requirements document or a design sprint. It developed iteratively across CDIL projects in which librarians worked as both technical developers and intellectual partners, building features to meet specific research questions and narrative needs and then generalizing them for reuse. Each project inherited recipes and code from the ones before it, along with the accumulated skill and confidence of the CDIL developers.

### Fire Lines (2022–23)

The first project to substantially shape what became CB-Essay was [Fire Lines](https://cdil.lib.uidaho.edu/fire-lines/), a creative nonfiction essay by Isabel Marlens, a University of Idaho MFA student, on the history of the 1910 Big Burn in the Northwest.

As part of the [Confluence Lab's](https://www.theconfluencelab.org/) Stories of Fire project, Marlens dug into U of I Special Collections to support a broader exploration of how cultural understandings of wildfire have changed over time. She began writing an essay that wove together personal and environmental narrative, Indigenous fire knowledge, Western science, and archival materials—photographs, maps, administrative records, newspaper clippings.

She built a curated collection of archival objects and scholarly sources—an exhibit, from a library perspective—but clearly in service of a narrative. The site took shape through her work with CDIL Co-Director Evan Peter Williamson, who built out the template alongside her essay. Starting from CollectionBuilder-CSV, they moved the "About" page content from the back to the front, so the site focused on the essay and the flow from chapter to chapter, with the collection underneath to support it. Essay sections are navigable alongside the underlying collection; a reader can follow the narrative thread or pivot into the collection to browse related items.

What Marlens and Williamson made here changed how we thought about the collection. It need not sit beneath the essay as a supplement; it could be part of the argument itself—evidence rather than decoration.

### Tender Spaces (2024)

The second shaping project was [Tender Spaces](https://cdil.lib.uidaho.edu/tender-spaces/), a five-part multilingual multimodal essay by Alicia Gladman, a CDIL Graduate Fellow and MFA student, on the life and forced institutionalization of the artist Gaëtane Buttigieg. Tender Spaces combines video interviews, photography, archival documents, and long-form writing in French and English, tracing Buttigieg's childhood, her coming out, and the harms she experienced at the hands of institutional psychiatry.

Where Fire Lines established a basic grammar of essay-integrated collection items, Tender Spaces pushed the template toward flexibility. Gladman's project started with the interviews she conducted in French with Gaëtane, her relative and an important queer role model in her life. From those interviews and her contextual research, she envisioned an interconnected group of essays that folded the interviews, their translations, and the supporting research into the text itself.

The vision took shape through her ongoing conversation with CDIL Co-Director Devin Becker, who worked through Gladman's ideas and customized specific features to realize them. Both wanted readers to be drawn through the narratives intellectually and visually. To do that, Becker extended the Fire Lines codebase with scroll-based interactions inside the essays, margin notes that add context or call up maps and diagrams, and video clips with toggle-able translations of the interview transcript. As those features developed, the template grew to accommodate not one way of presenting a multimodal essay but many, from a single codebase—each of the five essays unfolds differently as it moves down the page.

That range is itself the point. Tender Spaces showed that the paths a reader takes between narrative and source—how a translation surfaces, when a map appears, the order in which a video and its transcript meet the eye—are not cosmetic. They are part of the scholarly act, and a template should let an author shape them deliberately.

### Sedimentation (2025)

The third project that informed CB-Essay was [Sedimentation](https://cdil.lib.uidaho.edu/sedimentation/), the MA English thesis of Hannah Green, a multimedia exploration of Glen Canyon's entangled human and natural histories. Sedimentation organizes its content into five thematic essay "tributaries"—Atmosphere, Water, Biota, Humans, and Land—each woven through with archival photographs of the canyon before it was drowned beneath Lake Powell in 1963. The project also includes "Eddies," a choose-your-own-adventure reading mode that lets users move through interlinked story fragments in any order, building a nonlinear path through the canyon's histories.

Green's vision required new navigational structures. She wanted readers to move across the five tributaries—never stuck in one stream of thought, but able to follow another theme or juxtaposition by clicking a button at the bottom of a section. That meant building avenues across essays and, at the same time, features that oriented readers when they arrived somewhere new. It produced a section navigation autogenerated from the H2 elements on a page, and a gallery that keeps users in place while they examine an archival photograph closely, anchoring the collection in Green's prose rather than sending readers in and out of her work to see it.

Sedimentation also produced CB-Essay's auto-generated print layouts, built with Paged.js. Because the project was Green's MA thesis, we needed print artifacts her committee could read and annotate. Print output is usually at the mercy of the reader's browser settings; we wanted something purposeful instead. So we developed both the print layout styles for the essays and a print hub that lets a user print selected essays or assemble a full "book" by choosing all or some of them. The same source files that drive the website produce the typeset PDF—digital-first publication without duplicating the writing.

## The Collaboration Model

The three projects were not usability studies or requirements-gathering exercises. They were real scholarship, produced under real research conditions by graduate students working on MFA projects, MA theses, and CDIL fellowships with serious scholarly and creative ambitions. The features in CB-Essay came out of those genuine needs. The Liquid includes for inline image embeds exist because Marlens needed them for her wildfire essay. The print features exist because Green's thesis required readable print output. Iterating on the template through actual projects is what made the development work, and it is what we believe will make the template useful to others.

That kind of work requires librarians who hold two roles at once: technical developer and intellectual partner. Technically, it means Jekyll and Liquid templates, HTML and CSS, metadata schema design, and JavaScript for interactive components. Intellectually, it means engaging with research questions, archival materials, narrative structures, and the specific argument a scholar is making, and asking how the platform can serve that argument. At CDIL we do not separate these roles, and the combination is how we serve both our scholars and the wider users of our static web frameworks.

## Conclusion

CB-Essay came out of a simple conviction: the platform should do what the scholarship requires, not the reverse. Fire Lines, Tender Spaces, and Sedimentation each pushed that further, and the template is the accumulation of those demands.

Two things the projects taught us are worth stating plainly. Collections are not a supplement to the prose—they are evidence, and the platform should treat them that way. And the paths a reader can take between narrative and source are part of the argument, not decoration.

But the lessons matter less than how we arrived at them. We did not design a platform and then look for projects to fill it. We worked on three pieces of real scholarship, as developers and as partners, and let the template fall out of what those projects needed. That is a slower way to build a tool, and a less tidy one. It is also, we think, why the tool is any good—and why we would point other libraries toward the model before the template. The template is open and the code is documented, but the reproducible part is the collaboration.
